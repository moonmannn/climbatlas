# ClimbAtlas Content Patterns

## Shared workflow

Before drafting, identify the content mode, user decision, known facts, unknown facts, language, and available space. Do not fill missing evidence with atmosphere.

For bilingual work, establish one meaning brief first. Draft the stronger source language, then adapt the other language to the same promise, level of certainty, and personality. Review both together.

## 1. UI copy

**Purpose:** Help the user understand state or take an action.

- Use direct verbs and familiar nouns.
- Prefer `Save to Want to climb` over branded or metaphorical commands.
- Keep buttons short. Put explanation outside the button.
- Describe unavailable states plainly.
- Never show editorial workflow or import terminology.

## 2. Homepage product copy

**Purpose:** Explain what ClimbAtlas does and why it is useful.

Recommended sequence:

1. User outcome.
2. How the product helps.
3. One clear next action.

The homepage may be memorable, but should not promise perfect personalization or imply that a quiz replaces judgment.

## 3. Destination editorial copy

Answer these questions in order:

1. What defines the destination?
2. Who is it best for?
3. What does the climbing experience feel like?
4. What are the meaningful trade-offs?

Use atmosphere to make the choice legible, not to advertise the place. Mention crowds, weather dependence, style specificity, logistical complexity, or required research only when supported. Avoid turning a destination into a personality stereotype.

Suggested length: 45-90 words for an introduction; one focused idea per supporting paragraph.

## 4. Route card copy

Use this information hierarchy:

1. What kind of route is this?
2. Why is it notable?
3. Who may enjoy it?
4. What should the climber consider?

Keep factual fields factual. Keep editorial fit language clearly interpretive. A route card should generally contain no more than one metaphor, and no metaphor is required.

Avoid repeating the route name in every sentence. Vary the structure across neighboring cards while preserving scanability.

## 5. Route detail copy

Organize the page into distinct layers:

- **Facts:** sourced name, grade, type, length, sector, and status.
- **Why consider it:** concise ClimbAtlas judgment about fit.
- **Practice direction:** non-beta training themes only.
- **Sources and external resources:** what each source verifies and where the user can continue.
- **Unavailable information:** state the gap without apology or invention.

Do not transform route metadata into a narrative. Do not infer movement, protection, quality, popularity, or safety from a name, grade, or route type.

## 6. Climbing DNA quiz copy

- Ask one preference question at a time.
- Make answer options distinct without making one sound superior.
- Describe preferences, not competence or identity worth.
- Avoid leading users toward adventure, performance, or social answers.
- Keep option labels short and descriptions concrete.
- Do not use factual claims about a place unless the question cites or derives from verified data.

## 7. Climbing DNA archetype copy

Archetypes may be more expressive and identity-driven than the rest of the product. Still:

- Describe tendencies, not fixed identities.
- Use `you may prefer` and `you are drawn to` when certainty matters.
- Pair one memorable line with concrete preference dimensions.
- Do not imply ability, experience, bravery, or readiness.
- Do not make an archetype sound better than another.

## 8. DNA Match explanations

Explain the match through aligned dimensions, such as exploration, flow, performance, adventure, social energy, or comfort.

- Call the number a `preference match` when a percentage is shown.
- Name the strongest aligned dimensions.
- Name meaningful trade-offs or missing data.
- Mark inferred route attributes as inferred.
- Never frame a score as safety advice, grade readiness, or a prediction that the user will send or enjoy the route.

Preferred pattern:

> Strong alignment on exploration and longer objectives. The route profile is inferred from type, grade band, and length; current conditions and personal readiness are not included.

## 9. English and Chinese localization

- Preserve meaning, certainty, and personality rather than syntax.
- Write natural Chinese sentence order. Avoid imported English abstractions when a direct Chinese phrase exists.
- Keep proper route and destination names unless an established Chinese name is available.
- Use `线路` consistently for climbing routes in product copy; retain source terminology in factual citations when necessary.
- Use `难度` for grade in reader-facing Chinese and retain the original grading system alongside it.
- Translate product labels consistently: `preference match` as `偏好匹配`, `inferred` as `推断`, and `source` as `来源`.
- Avoid mixed-language filler such as `session`, `ego`, or `card` when natural Chinese is available.
- Treat replacement characters, mojibake, or malformed punctuation as blocking defects.

## Final check

Before shipping, ask:

- Does this help a climber decide something?
- Can every factual claim be traced?
- Are judgment and inference labeled honestly?
- Is any sentence cleverer than it is useful?
- Do English and Chinese make the same promise?
- Is any internal workflow language visible?
